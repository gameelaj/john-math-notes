Simultaneous Equations: Elimination & Substitution

📅 30 April 2026 IGCSE Edexcel · Algebra · Paper 1H & 2H

What's the idea? A simultaneous equation is when you have two equations with two unknowns (usually x and y). One equation alone has infinite solutions — but two equations together pin down one specific pair of values that works for both.

Why it matters: Almost every "real-world" maths problem (mixing prices, ages, rates, intersections of lines) becomes a simultaneous equation. Master this and you unlock half of Paper 2H.

📐 Method 1: Elimination

The big idea: get rid of one variable by adding or subtracting the two equations. Whatever's left is a normal one-variable equation you already know how to solve.

1
Reorder so the variables line up
Both equations should have x on one side, y next to it, and the number on the other side. Same column = same variable.
2
Make the coefficients of x or y the same
Pick the variable that's easier to match. Multiply one (or both) equations by a number until the coefficients match. Multiply every term, not just one!
3
Add or subtract the equations (stacked)
SSS = Same Sign Subtract. If the matching coefficients have the same sign (both +3y or both −3y) → subtract. If they have different signs (+3y and −3y) → add. Stack the equations one above the other and work column by column.
4
Solve the one-variable equation
After step 3, one variable disappears. You're left with something like 5x = 20 → divide both sides by 5 → x = 4.
5
Substitute back to find the other variable
Plug the value you found into either original equation. Pick the simpler one.
6
Check by plugging both values into the other equation
30 seconds of checking saves you from losing 4 marks. Always do it.
💡 Memory hook: "Same Signs Subtract" (SSS). If the signs match, subtract. If they're different, add. Whisper "SSS" before step 3 every time.

Worked Examples — Elimination

Easy
Solve: 2x + y = 7 and 3x − y = 8
(1) 2x + y = 7
(2) 3x − y = 8
Already lined up nicely. Look at y: coefficient is +1 in (1) and −1 in (2).
Different signs → ADD the two equations. Stack them:
(1)2x + y = 7
+ (2)3x − y = 8
5x + 0 = 15
Now solve 5x = 15. Divide both sides by 5:
5x = 15
5x ÷ 5 = 15 ÷ 5
x = 3
Substitute x = 3 into equation (1) to find y:
2x + y = 7
2(3) + y = 7
6 + y = 7
6 + y − 6 = 7 − 6
y = 1
Check in equation (2): 3(3) − 1 = 9 − 1 = 8 ✓
x = 3, y = 1
Easy
Solve: 4x + 3y = 18 and 4x − y = 6
(1) 4x + 3y = 18
(2) 4x − y = 6
Coefficients of x are both +4 — same sign → SUBTRACT. Do (1) − (2):
(1)4x + 3y = 18
− (2)4x − y = 6
0 + 4y = 12
Watch the y column carefully: 3y − (−y) = 3y + y = 4y. The double-negative is where most marks get dropped.
Solve 4y = 12. Divide both sides by 4:
4y = 12
4y ÷ 4 = 12 ÷ 4
y = 3
Substitute y = 3 into equation (2):
4x − y = 6
4x − 3 = 6
4x − 3 + 3 = 6 + 3
4x = 9
4x ÷ 4 = 9 ÷ 4
x = 9/4 = 2.25
Check in (1): 4(2.25) + 3(3) = 9 + 9 = 18 ✓
x = 9/4 (or 2.25), y = 3
Medium
Solve: 3x + 2y = 16 and 5x − 3y = 14
(1) 3x + 2y = 16
(2) 5x − 3y = 14
Coefficients don't match anywhere. To match y, find the LCM of 2 and 3 = 6.
Multiply (1) by 3 (so 2y becomes 6y), and (2) by 2 (so 3y becomes 6y). Multiply every term:
(1) × 3: 3x × 3 + 2y × 3 = 16 × 3
9x + 6y = 48 ... call this (3)
(2) × 2: 5x × 2 − 3y × 2 = 14 × 2
10x − 6y = 28 ... call this (4)
Now y coefficients are +6 and −6. Different signs → ADD. Stack (3) and (4):
(3) 9x + 6y = 48
+ (4)10x − 6y = 28
19x + 0 = 76
Solve 19x = 76:
19x = 76
19x ÷ 19 = 76 ÷ 19
x = 4
Substitute x = 4 into equation (1):
3x + 2y = 16
3(4) + 2y = 16
12 + 2y = 16
12 + 2y − 12 = 16 − 12
2y = 4
2y ÷ 2 = 4 ÷ 2
y = 2
Check in (2): 5(4) − 3(2) = 20 − 6 = 14 ✓
x = 4, y = 2
Hard — Exam style
A shop sells 4 pens and 3 notebooks for £11. It sells 6 pens and 2 notebooks for £10. Find the price of each.
Let p = price of one pen, n = price of one notebook. Turn the words into equations:
(1) 4p + 3n = 11
(2) 6p + 2n = 10
To match n, find LCM of 3 and 2 = 6. Multiply (1) by 2 and (2) by 3:
(1) × 2: 4p × 2 + 3n × 2 = 11 × 2
8p + 6n = 22 ... call this (3)
(2) × 3: 6p × 3 + 2n × 3 = 10 × 3
18p + 6n = 30 ... call this (4)
Same sign on n (+6 and +6) → SUBTRACT. Do (4) − (3) so the p number stays positive:
(4)18p + 6n = 30
− (3) 8p + 6n = 22
10p + 0 = 8
Solve 10p = 8:
10p = 8
10p ÷ 10 = 8 ÷ 10
p = 0.80
Substitute p = 0.80 into equation (1):
4p + 3n = 11
4(0.80) + 3n = 11
3.20 + 3n = 11
3.20 + 3n − 3.20 = 11 − 3.20
3n = 7.80
3n ÷ 3 = 7.80 ÷ 3
n = 2.60
Check in (2): 6(0.80) + 2(2.60) = 4.80 + 5.20 = 10 ✓
Pen = £0.80, Notebook = £2.60

🔄 Method 2: Substitution

The big idea: rearrange one equation to make x or y the subject, then plug that expression into the other equation. You replace one letter with an expression — substitution!

1
Pick the easier equation to rearrange
Look for a variable with coefficient 1 (or −1) — it rearranges cleanly with no fractions.
2
Rearrange to make x or y the subject
e.g. y = 2x + 3 (or x = ...). Now you have an expression for that variable.
3
Substitute into the OTHER equation
Don't substitute back into the same equation — you'll get 0 = 0, which is useless. Always sub into the equation you didn't rearrange.
4
Solve the resulting one-variable equation
Expand brackets carefully, collect like terms, solve.
5
Substitute back to find the other variable
Use your rearranged expression from step 2 — that's the quickest way.

Worked Examples — Substitution (Linear)

Easy
Solve: y = 3x − 1 and 2x + y = 9
(1) y = 3x − 1
(2) 2x + y = 9
Equation (1) is already rearranged: y is by itself. Substitute the expression "3x − 1" wherever y appears in equation (2):
2x + y = 9
2x + (3x − 1) = 9
2x + 3x − 1 = 9
5x − 1 = 9
5x − 1 + 1 = 9 + 1
5x = 10
5x ÷ 5 = 10 ÷ 5
x = 2
Substitute x = 2 back into equation (1):
y = 3x − 1
y = 3(2) − 1
y = 6 − 1
y = 5
Check in (2): 2(2) + 5 = 4 + 5 = 9 ✓
x = 2, y = 5
Medium
Solve: x + 2y = 11 and 3x − y = 5
(1) x + 2y = 11
(2) 3x − y = 5
Equation (1) has x with coefficient 1 — easiest to rearrange. Make x the subject:
x + 2y = 11
x + 2y − 2y = 11 − 2y
x = 11 − 2y
Substitute "11 − 2y" wherever x appears in equation (2):
3x − y = 5
3(11 − 2y) − y = 5
Expand the brackets carefully — multiply 3 by both terms inside:
3 × 11 − 3 × 2y − y = 5
33 − 6y − y = 5
Collect the y terms: −6y − y = −7y.
33 − 7y = 5
33 − 7y − 33 = 5 − 33
−7y = −28
−7y ÷ (−7) = −28 ÷ (−7)
y = 4
Substitute y = 4 back into x = 11 − 2y:
x = 11 − 2(4)
x = 11 − 8
x = 3
Check in (2): 3(3) − 4 = 9 − 4 = 5 ✓
x = 3, y = 4

🚀 Why substitution is more powerful

Here's the cool part: elimination only works when both equations are linear (just x and y, no x² or xy or anything fancy). But substitution works on non-linear systems too — like when one equation is a curve.

This is huge for IGCSE: questions about a line meeting a circle, or a line meeting a quadratic curve, are guaranteed Paper 2H material. Elimination can't do these. Substitution can.

Worked Examples — Substitution (Non-linear)

Medium
Solve: y = x + 1 and y = x² − 1
(1) y = x + 1
(2) y = x² − 1
Both equations equal y, so the right-hand sides must equal each other:
x + 1 = x² − 1
Rearrange so everything is on one side (= 0). Subtract (x + 1) from both sides:
x + 1 − (x + 1) = x² − 1 − (x + 1)
0 = x² − 1 − x − 1
0 = x² − x − 2
x² − x − 2 = 0
Factorise the quadratic. Find two numbers that multiply to −2 and add to −1: those are −2 and +1.
(x − 2)(x + 1) = 0
Either bracket can be zero:
x − 2 = 0 → x = 2
x + 1 = 0 → x = −1
Two x-values means two solutions — the line crosses the curve at two points. Find y for each:
When x = 2: y = x + 1 = 2 + 1 = 3
When x = −1: y = x + 1 = −1 + 1 = 0
Check (2, 3) in equation (2): y = x² − 1 = 4 − 1 = 3 ✓
Check (−1, 0) in equation (2): y = x² − 1 = 1 − 1 = 0 ✓
(x = 2, y = 3) and (x = −1, y = 0)
Hard — Exam style
Solve: x² + y² = 25 and y = x + 1
(1) x² + y² = 25
(2) y = x + 1
Equation (1) is a circle of radius 5. Equation (2) is a straight line. We're finding where they cross.
Substitute y = x + 1 into equation (1) — replace y with (x + 1):
x² + y² = 25
x² + (x + 1)² = 25
Expand (x + 1)² carefully. It is NOT x² + 1. Use FOIL or "smile":
(x + 1)² = (x + 1)(x + 1)
= x·x + x·1 + 1·x + 1·1
= x² + x + x + 1
= x² + 2x + 1
Now substitute that expansion back in:
x² + (x² + 2x + 1) = 25
x² + x² + 2x + 1 = 25
2x² + 2x + 1 = 25
Move 25 to the left side to get = 0:
2x² + 2x + 1 − 25 = 25 − 25
2x² + 2x − 24 = 0
Divide every term by 2 to simplify:
2x² ÷ 2 + 2x ÷ 2 − 24 ÷ 2 = 0
x² + x − 12 = 0
Factorise. Two numbers that multiply to −12 and add to +1: those are +4 and −3.
(x + 4)(x − 3) = 0
x + 4 = 0 → x = −4
x − 3 = 0 → x = 3
Find y for each using y = x + 1:
When x = −4: y = −4 + 1 = −3
When x = 3: y = 3 + 1 = 4
Check (−4, −3) in (1): (−4)² + (−3)² = 16 + 9 = 25 ✓
Check (3, 4) in (1): 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 ✓
(x = −4, y = −3) and (x = 3, y = 4)

⚠️ Common Mistakes John Should Watch For

1. Forgetting to multiply EVERY term. If you multiply equation (1) by 3, every single term gets ×3, including the number on the right side. Easy 2 marks lost.
2. Mixing up add/subtract. Same Signs Subtract (SSS). Different signs add. Whisper SSS before step 3, every time.
3. Double negatives in the y column. When subtracting, 3y − (−y) = 3y + y = 4y, not 2y. Two negatives make a positive.
4. Substituting back into the wrong equation. When you rearrange equation (1), you must sub into equation (2). Subbing back into (1) just gives 0 = 0 — useless.
5. Squaring brackets wrong. (x + 1)² = x² + 2x + 1, NOT x² + 1. Always expand by writing out (x + 1)(x + 1) and using FOIL.
6. Forgetting the second solution in non-linear systems. A line crossing a curve usually meets it at TWO points. If you only give one (x, y) pair, you lose half the marks.

⚡ Quick Recap (30-second review)

  • Elimination: match coefficients → stack equations → SSS (Same Signs Subtract, different signs add) → solve → sub back.
  • Substitution: rearrange one equation to get y = ... or x = ... → sub that expression into the OTHER equation → solve → sub back.
  • Multiply every term when scaling an equation.
  • Substitution is the more powerful method — it handles non-linear systems (line + circle, line + curve).
  • Non-linear systems usually have two solutions (two intersection points). Always check for both.
  • Always verify your answer by plugging both values into the equation you didn't use to solve.

7 Word problem: Two coffees and three teas cost £8.50. Three coffees and two teas cost £9. Find the price of each. Hard
Worked solution
Let c = price of one coffee, t = price of one tea. Turn the words into equations:
(1) 2c + 3t = 8.50
(2) 3c + 2t = 9
Match c: LCM of 2 and 3 = 6. Multiply (1) by 3 and (2) by 2.
(1) × 3: 2c × 3 + 3t × 3 = 8.50 × 3
6c + 9t = 25.50 ... call this (3)
(2) × 2: 3c × 2 + 2t × 2 = 9 × 2
6c + 4t = 18 ... call this (4)
Same sign on c → SUBTRACT. Do (3) − (4):
(3)6c + 9t = 25.50
− (4)6c + 4t = 18.00
0 + 5t = 7.50
5t = 7.50
5t ÷ 5 = 7.50 ÷ 5
t = 1.50

8 Solve (non-linear): y = x + 2 and y = x² − 4 Hard
Worked solution
(1) y = x + 2
(2) y = x² − 4
Both equations equal y, so the right-hand sides must be equal:
x + 2 = x² − 4
Get everything on one side. Subtract (x + 2) from both sides:
x + 2 − (x + 2) = x² − 4 − (x + 2)
0 = x² − 4 − x − 2

(x − 3)(x + 2) = 0
x − 3 = 0 → x = 3

When x = 3: y = 3 + 2 = 5
When x = −2: y = −2 + 2 = 0
Check (3, 5) in (2): y = 3² − 4 = 9 − 4 = 5 ✓
Check (−2, 0) in (2): y = (−2)² − 4 = 4 − 4 = 0 ✓
