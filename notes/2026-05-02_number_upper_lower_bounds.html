Upper & Lower Bounds

📅 2 May 2026 📌 Edexcel IGCSE — Number 📄 Paper 1H & 2H ⚖️ Limits of Accuracy

💡 What & Why

Whenever a measurement is rounded, the true value could be slightly bigger or slightly smaller than what's written. The upper bound is the largest the value could possibly be, and the lower bound is the smallest it could be.

This matters in real life — if you're building something, a tiny rounding error could make it too big or too small. Edexcel tests this because it's a core number skill for Higher tier and appears regularly on both papers, often worth 3–4 marks.

There are two kinds of question: (1) finding bounds from a rounded number, and (2) using bounds in calculations (area, speed, difference, etc.). This note covers both.

📐 Part 1 — Finding Upper & Lower Bounds

THE KEY RULE — always half the unit of rounding
Whatever you've rounded to, add half of it to get the upper bound, and subtract half of it to get the lower bound.
Step 1 — Identify the unit of rounding
Read the question carefully. It tells you: "nearest cm", "nearest 10", "2 d.p.", "3 s.f.", etc. From that, find the smallest unit.
Step 2 — Halve the unit
Divide that unit by 2. This is called the half-interval.
Step 3 — Add and subtract
Upper bound = given value + half-interval
Lower bound = given value − half-interval
Warning — Upper bound is NEVER reached
The upper bound is the value the measurement approaches but never quite reaches. That's why error intervals are written as: LB ≤ x < UB (strict inequality at the top). This is very important for the error interval notation question.

Quick Reference — Common Rounding Scenarios

Rounded to… Unit Half-interval Example: value = 24
Nearest 1 (integer) 1 0.5 LB = 23.5   UB = 24.5
Nearest 10 10 5 e.g. 240: LB = 235   UB = 245
Nearest 100 100 50 e.g. 2400: LB = 2350   UB = 2450
1 decimal place (0.1) 0.1 0.05 e.g. 6.4: LB = 6.35   UB = 6.45
2 decimal places (0.01) 0.01 0.005 e.g. 3.82: LB = 3.815   UB = 3.825
3 significant figures Depends on size Half the last sig. fig. position e.g. 24.8: LB = 24.75   UB = 24.85
Nearest 5 5 2.5 e.g. 145: LB = 142.5   UB = 147.5
Error Interval Notation
Always write bounds using:   LB ≤ x < UB
Example: 6.4 rounded to 1 d.p. → 6.35 ≤ x < 6.45
Note the ≤ on the left (LB is included) and < on the right (UB is NOT included).

🗝️ Part 2 — Using Bounds in Calculations: The Golden Rules

This is the part most people get wrong. The rule is: think about what makes the answer as big/small as possible.

Calculation For MAX (Upper Bound) For MIN (Lower Bound) Memory Hook
Addition
A + B		 UB(A) + UB(B) LB(A) + LB(B) Both big → bigger sum
Both small → smaller sum
Subtraction
A − B		 UB(A) − LB(B) LB(A) − UB(B) Big minus small → biggest difference
"SSS" — Same Signs Subtract!
(to find UB: use opposite signs)
Multiplication
A × B
(area, volume)		 UB(A) × UB(B) LB(A) × LB(B) Both big → biggest product
Both small → smallest product
Division
A ÷ B
(speed = dist ÷ time)		 UB(A) ÷ LB(B) LB(A) ÷ UB(B) Big ÷ small → biggest result
Small ÷ big → smallest result
Perimeter
(sum of sides)		 UB of every side LB of every side Same as addition — all big or all small
🔍 Context 1: Finding Bounds & Error Intervals
EASY Nearest whole number Example 1 — Simple bounds from rounding to nearest cm
A pencil measures 14 cm, correct to the nearest centimetre. Write down the upper and lower bounds for its length, and write the error interval.
Step 1 — What was it rounded to?
Nearest centimetre → the unit is 1 cm.
Step 2 — Find the half-interval
Half of 1 cm = 0.5 cm
Step 3 — Add and subtract
Upper bound = 14 + 0.5 = 14.5 cm Lower bound = 14 − 0.5 = 13.5 cm
Step 4 — Write the error interval
13.5 ≤ length < 14.5
✓ SENSE CHECK: Any value from 13.5 up to (but not including) 14.5 rounds to 14. Correct! ✔
EASY Nearest 10 Example 2 — Bounds from rounding to the nearest 10
A crowd of 4800 people is given correct to the nearest 100. Write down the error interval for the number of people, n.
Step 1 — Unit of rounding
Nearest 100 → unit is 100.
Step 2 — Half-interval
Half of 100 = 50
Step 3 — Upper and lower bounds
Upper bound = 4800 + 50 = 4850 Lower bound = 4800 − 50 = 4750
Step 4 — Error interval
4750 ≤ n < 4850
✓ SENSE CHECK: 4750 rounds up to 4800 ✔   4849 rounds down to 4800 ✔   4850 would round up to 4900, so it's excluded. ✔
EASY Significant figures Example 3 — Bounds from significant figures
A speed is recorded as 24.8 m/s, correct to 3 significant figures. Find the upper and lower bounds.
Step 1 — What is the last significant figure's place?
3 significant figures in 24.8 → the last sig. fig. is the digit 8, which is in the tenths column (1 decimal place). So the unit of rounding is 0.1.
Step 2 — Half-interval
Half of 0.1 = 0.05
Step 3 — Bounds
Upper bound = 24.8 + 0.05 = 24.85 m/s Lower bound = 24.8 − 0.05 = 24.75 m/s
Step 4 — Error interval
24.75 ≤ speed < 24.85
📏 Context 2: Perimeter
EASY Perimeter Example 4 — Upper bound for perimeter of a rectangle
A rectangle has length 35 cm and width 26 cm, each measured to the nearest centimetre. Calculate the upper bound for the perimeter.
Step 1 — Find bounds for each measurement
Length: rounded to nearest 1 cm → half-interval = 0.5 cm UB(length) = 35 + 0.5 = 35.5 cm LB(length) = 35 − 0.5 = 34.5 cm Width: rounded to nearest 1 cm → half-interval = 0.5 cm UB(width) = 26 + 0.5 = 26.5 cm LB(width) = 26 − 0.5 = 25.5 cm
Step 2 — Rule: UB of perimeter = UB of ALL sides
To make the perimeter as large as possible, use the upper bound for every measurement.
Step 3 — Calculate upper bound of perimeter
Perimeter = 2 × (length + width) UB(P) = 2 × (UB(length) + UB(width)) UB(P) = 2 × (35.5 + 26.5) UB(P) = 2 × 62 UB(P) = 124 cm
✓ CHECK: Nominal perimeter = 2 × (35 + 26) = 122 cm. Our answer of 124 cm is slightly above that — makes sense! ✔
🟦 Context 3: Area
MEDIUM Area — rectangle Example 5 — Upper and lower bounds for area of a rectangle
A field has width 28 m (nearest metre) and length 145 m (nearest 5 metres). Calculate the upper and lower bounds for the area of the field.
Step 1 — Bounds for width (nearest 1 metre)
Half-interval = 0.5 m UB(width) = 28 + 0.5 = 28.5 m LB(width) = 28 − 0.5 = 27.5 m
Step 2 — Bounds for length (nearest 5 metres)
Half-interval = 5 ÷ 2 = 2.5 m UB(length) = 145 + 2.5 = 147.5 m LB(length) = 145 − 2.5 = 142.5 m
Step 3 — Rule: for area (multiplication)
UB of area = UB × UB     LB of area = LB × LB
Step 4 — Calculate UB of area
UB(area) = UB(width) × UB(length) UB(area) = 28.5 × 147.5 28.5 × 147.5 ─────── 142.5 (28.5 × 5 ÷ 10) 1995.0 (28.5 × 70) 2280.0 (28.5 × 80) + 28.5 × 100 = 2850 ─────────────────── 4203.75 m² UB(area) = 4203.75 m²
Step 5 — Calculate LB of area
LB(area) = LB(width) × LB(length) LB(area) = 27.5 × 142.5 27.5 × 142.5 ─────── 137.5 (27.5 × 5 ÷ 10) 1925.0 (27.5 × 70) 2200.0 (27.5 × 80) + 27.5 × 100 = 2750 ────────────────── 3918.75 m² LB(area) = 3918.75 m²
✓ CHECK: Nominal area = 28 × 145 = 4060 m². Our UB (4203.75) is above and LB (3918.75) is below — that's correct! ✔
➖ Context 4: Difference (Subtraction)
Why subtraction is the trickiest one
To get the maximum difference (UB of A − B), you want A as big as possible AND B as small as possible. This feels backwards! Memory hook: "Big minus small = biggest gap"
MEDIUM Difference Example 6 — Maximum and minimum difference between two lengths
Rope A is 5.6 m long, correct to 1 decimal place. Rope B is 3.2 m long, correct to 1 decimal place. Find the upper and lower bounds for the difference in their lengths (A − B).
Step 1 — Bounds for Rope A (1 d.p. → unit 0.1, half = 0.05)
UB(A) = 5.6 + 0.05 = 5.65 m LB(A) = 5.6 − 0.05 = 5.55 m
Step 2 — Bounds for Rope B (1 d.p. → unit 0.1, half = 0.05)
UB(B) = 3.2 + 0.05 = 3.25 m LB(B) = 3.2 − 0.05 = 3.15 m
Step 3 — RULE for subtraction
UB(A − B) = UB(A) − LB(B)   ← big minus small = biggest difference
LB(A − B) = LB(A) − UB(B)   ← small minus big = smallest difference
Step 4 — Upper bound of difference
UB(A − B) = UB(A) − LB(B) = 5.65 − 3.15 = 2.50 m
Step 5 — Lower bound of difference
LB(A − B) = LB(A) − UB(B) = 5.55 − 3.25 = 2.30 m
✓ CHECK: Nominal difference = 5.6 − 3.2 = 2.4 m. Our UB (2.5) is above and LB (2.3) is below. Correct! ✔
🚗 Context 5: Speed = Distance ÷ Time (Division)
Why division is tricky
To maximise a fraction, you want big numerator ÷ small denominator. So UB(speed) = UB(distance) ÷ LB(time). Think: if you go far and take less time, your speed is highest.
MEDIUM Speed — division Example 7 — Upper bound for average speed
A car travels 240 km, correct to the nearest 10 km, in 3.5 hours, correct to 1 decimal place. Find the upper bound for the average speed of the car.
Step 1 — Bounds for distance (nearest 10 km)
Half-interval = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 km UB(distance) = 240 + 5 = 245 km LB(distance) = 240 − 5 = 235 km
Step 2 — Bounds for time (1 d.p., unit = 0.1 h)
Half-interval = 0.1 ÷ 2 = 0.05 hours UB(time) = 3.5 + 0.05 = 3.55 hours LB(time) = 3.5 − 0.05 = 3.45 hours
Step 3 — Rule: UB(speed) = UB(distance) ÷ LB(time)
More distance in less time → fastest possible speed.
Step 4 — Calculate upper bound of speed
UB(speed) = UB(distance) ÷ LB(time) = 245 ÷ 3.45 245 ÷ 3.45 = 71.014... UB(speed) = 71.0 km/h (to 3 s.f.)
✓ CHECK: Nominal speed = 240 ÷ 3.5 = 68.6 km/h. Our upper bound (71.0) is higher — correct! ✔
🎯 Exam-Style Multi-Step Questions
HARD Formula with division — exam style Example 8 — Formula: Petrol consumption = Distance ÷ Volume
A car travels 150 miles, measured to the nearest 10 miles. It uses 12.4 litres of petrol, measured to 3 significant figures. Calculate the upper bound for the car's petrol consumption in miles per litre. Give your answer correct to 2 decimal places.
Step 1 — Bounds for distance (nearest 10 miles)
Half-interval = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 miles UB(distance) = 150 + 5 = 155 miles LB(distance) = 150 − 5 = 145 miles
Step 2 — Bounds for volume (3 s.f.: last digit is in tenths → unit = 0.1)
Half-interval = 0.1 ÷ 2 = 0.05 litres UB(volume) = 12.4 + 0.05 = 12.45 litres LB(volume) = 12.4 − 0.05 = 12.35 litres
Step 3 — Rule: consumption = distance ÷ volume (division)
UB(consumption) = UB(distance) ÷ LB(volume)
More distance on less fuel → highest consumption figure.
Step 4 — Calculate
UB(consumption) = 155 ÷ 12.35 = 12.55... Let's work this out carefully: 12.35 × 12 = 148.2 155 − 148.2 = 6.8 remaining 6.8 ÷ 12.35 = 0.550... So: 155 ÷ 12.35 = 12.550... UB(consumption) = 12.55 miles per litre (to 2 d.p.)
✓ CHECK: Nominal = 150 ÷ 12.4 = 12.10 miles/litre. Upper bound (12.55) is above this — correct! ✔
HARD Mixed — area of annulus (ring) Example 9 — Bounds within a formula: area of a ring shape
A ring shape (annulus) has outer radius R = 8.5 cm and inner radius r = 3.2 cm, each correct to 1 decimal place. The area formula is A = π(R² − r²). Find the lower bound for the area of the ring.
Step 1 — Bounds for R and r (1 d.p.)
Half-interval for both = 0.05 cm UB(R) = 8.5 + 0.05 = 8.55 cm LB(R) = 8.5 − 0.05 = 8.45 cm UB(r) = 3.2 + 0.05 = 3.25 cm LB(r) = 3.2 − 0.05 = 3.15 cm
Step 2 — Think carefully about what MINIMISES the area
Area = π(R² − r²). This is a subtraction: R² minus r². To get the minimum area, we want R² as small as possible (so use LB of R) AND r² as large as possible (so use UB of r). This is the subtraction rule again!
Step 3 — Calculate LB(R²) using LB(R)
LB(R²) = LB(R) × LB(R) = 8.45 × 8.45 = 71.4025 cm²
Step 4 — Calculate UB(r²) using UB(r)
UB(r²) = UB(r) × UB(r) = 3.25 × 3.25 = 10.5625 cm²
Step 5 — Calculate LB of area
LB(A) = π × (LB(R²) − UB(r²)) = π × (71.4025 − 10.5625) = π × 60.84 = 191.13... cm² LB(area) = 191.1 cm² (to 1 d.p.)
✓ CHECK: Nominal area = π × (8.5² − 3.2²) = π × (72.25 − 10.24) = π × 62.01 = 194.8 cm². Our lower bound (191.1) is below this — correct! ✔
HARD Speed, distance, time — prove speed limit breached Example 10 — Exam reasoning question
A road is 4530 m long, correct to the nearest 10 metres. Kirsty drove along the road in 205 seconds, correct to the nearest 5 seconds. The speed limit is 80 km/h. Could Kirsty have been breaking the speed limit? Show your working and justify your answer.
Step 1 — Bounds for distance (nearest 10 m)
Half-interval = 5 m UB(distance) = 4530 + 5 = 4535 m LB(distance) = 4530 − 5 = 4525 m
Step 2 — Bounds for time (nearest 5 s)
Half-interval = 2.5 s UB(time) = 205 + 2.5 = 207.5 s LB(time) = 205 − 2.5 = 202.5 s
Step 3 — To check if limit is BROKEN, find MAXIMUM possible speed
UB(speed) = UB(distance) ÷ LB(time) — this gives the fastest she could have gone.
Step 4 — Calculate UB of speed in m/s then convert to km/h
UB(speed) = 4535 ÷ 202.5 = 22.395... m/s Convert to km/h: 22.395... × 3600 ÷ 1000 = 22.395 × 3.6 = 80.622... km/h
Step 5 — Conclusion (must be written clearly for the mark!)
The maximum possible speed is 80.6 km/h. Since this is greater than the 80 km/h limit, it is possible that Kirsty was breaking the speed limit. However, it's also possible she wasn't — we cannot be certain.
✓ EXAM TIP: Always write a conclusion sentence in bounds reasoning questions. Saying "yes" or "no" alone gets no marks — you must explain with reference to your calculated bound. ✔

🚨 Common Mistakes — Watch Out, John!

❌
Subtracting the wrong bounds: For A − B, the upper bound is UB(A) − LB(B), NOT UB(A) − UB(B). Use "big minus small = biggest gap". This is the single most common error in bounds questions.
❌
Using the wrong half-interval for "nearest 5": If rounded to the nearest 5, the half-interval is 2.5, not 0.5. Always halve the rounding unit — not just assume 0.5.
❌
Writing UB with ≤ instead of <: The error interval is LB ≤ x < UB. The upper bound is never actually reached. Writing UB with ≤ loses a mark.
❌
Division: using UB ÷ UB for the upper bound: For UB(A ÷ B), you need UB(A) ÷ LB(B). A bigger denominator makes the fraction smaller, so you must use LB of the denominator to maximise the result.
❌
Not converting units before comparing: In speed questions, make sure both speeds are in the same units (both km/h, or both m/s) before comparing to a speed limit.
❌
Rounding mid-calculation: Never round until the very last step. If you round UB(A) or LB(B) before subtracting/dividing, your answer will be wrong. Keep all digits until the end.
❌
Forgetting the conclusion sentence: "Reasoning" or "could it exceed X?" questions require a written conclusion to earn the final mark. Always end with a clear sentence like "Therefore, it is possible that…"

⚡ Quick Recap — 30 Second Review

  • UB = value + half the rounding unit.   LB = value − half the rounding unit.
  • Error interval: LB ≤ x < UB — strict inequality (<) at the top.
  • Add/multiply: UB of result → use UB of everything. LB of result → use LB of everything.
  • Subtract (A − B): UB → UB(A) − LB(B). LB → LB(A) − UB(B). Memory: "big minus small = biggest gap".
  • Divide (A ÷ B): UB → UB(A) ÷ LB(B). LB → LB(A) ÷ UB(B). Memory: "big over small = biggest result".
  • Always write a conclusion sentence for reasoning questions. Never round mid-calculation.

1 A book weighs 340 g, correct to the nearest 10 g. Write the error interval for the weight, w. Easy ▾
Rounded to nearest 10 g → unit = 10, half-interval = 5 g UB = 340 + 5 = 345 g LB = 340 − 5 = 335 g Error interval: 335 ≤ w < 345
2 A plank is 2.3 m long, correct to 1 decimal place. Write down the upper and lower bounds. Easy ▾
1 d.p. → unit = 0.1 m, half-interval = 0.05 m UB = 2.3 + 0.05 = 2.35 m LB = 2.3 − 0.05 = 2.25 m Error interval: 2.25 ≤ length < 2.35
3 A number is given as 5700, correct to the nearest 100. Write the error interval. Easy ▾
Nearest 100 → unit = 100, half-interval = 50 UB = 5700 + 50 = 5750 LB = 5700 − 50 = 5650 Error interval: 5650 ≤ x < 5750
4 A rectangle is 9.4 cm × 6.1 cm, each to 1 d.p. Find the upper bound for the perimeter. Easy ▾
Both to 1 d.p. → half-interval = 0.05 cm UB(length) = 9.4 + 0.05 = 9.45 cm UB(width) = 6.1 + 0.05 = 6.15 cm UB(perimeter) = 2 × (UB(length) + UB(width)) = 2 × (9.45 + 6.15) = 2 × 15.60 = 31.2 cm Nominal perimeter = 2 × (9.4 + 6.1) = 31.0 cm ✔ (UB is above this)
5 A rectangle is 12 cm × 7 cm, each to the nearest cm. Find the lower bound for the area. Medium ▾
Nearest 1 cm → half-interval = 0.5 cm LB(length) = 12 − 0.5 = 11.5 cm LB(width) = 7 − 0.5 = 6.5 cm LB(area) = LB(length) × LB(width) = 11.5 × 6.5 = 74.75 cm² Nominal area = 12 × 7 = 84 cm² LB (74.75) is below this ✔
6 Box A weighs 8.6 kg and Box B weighs 3.9 kg, both correct to 1 d.p. Find the upper and lower bounds for the difference in their weights. Medium ▾
1 d.p. → half-interval = 0.05 kg UB(A) = 8.65 kg LB(A) = 8.55 kg UB(B) = 3.95 kg LB(B) = 3.85 kg Rule for subtraction: UB(A − B) = UB(A) − LB(B) = 8.65 − 3.85 = 4.80 kg LB(A − B) = LB(A) − UB(B) = 8.55 − 3.95 = 4.60 kg Nominal difference = 8.6 − 3.9 = 4.7 kg UB (4.80) above, LB (4.60) below ✔
7 A runner covers 400 m (nearest 10 m) in 52.4 s (nearest 0.1 s). Find the upper bound for their average speed in m/s. Give your answer to 3 s.f. Medium ▾
Distance: nearest 10 m → half = 5 UB(d) = 405 m Time: 1 d.p. → half = 0.05 s LB(t) = 52.4 − 0.05 = 52.35 s UB(speed) = UB(d) ÷ LB(t) = 405 ÷ 52.35 = 7.737... m/s = 7.74 m/s (3 s.f.) Nominal speed = 400 ÷ 52.4 = 7.63 m/s UB (7.74) is above ✔
8 A wire is 75 cm long to the nearest cm. It is cut into 6 equal pieces (6 is exact). Find the upper and lower bounds for the length of each piece. Medium ▾
Nearest 1 cm → half-interval = 0.5 cm UB(wire) = 75.5 cm LB(wire) = 74.5 cm Note: 6 is EXACT, so it has no bounds — only the wire length has uncertainty. UB(each piece) = UB(wire) ÷ 6 = 75.5 ÷ 6 = 12.5833... cm ≈ 12.58 cm (4 s.f.) LB(each piece) = LB(wire) ÷ 6 = 74.5 ÷ 6 = 12.4166... cm ≈ 12.42 cm (4 s.f.) Nominal = 75 ÷ 6 = 12.5 cm ✔

⚠️ Key point: exact numbers (like 6 pieces) have NO bounds — only measured quantities have bounds.

9 A train travels 320 km (nearest 10 km) in 2.5 hours (nearest 0.1 hours). The speed limit is 130 km/h. Could the train have exceeded the speed limit? Show full working. Hard ▾
Distance: nearest 10 km → half = 5 km UB(d) = 325 km LB(d) = 315 km Time: 1 d.p. → half = 0.05 h UB(t) = 2.55 h LB(t) = 2.45 h To check if limit exceeded → find UB(speed): UB(speed) = UB(d) ÷ LB(t) = 325 ÷ 2.45 = 132.653... km/h

The maximum possible speed is 132.7 km/h, which exceeds 130 km/h. Therefore, it is possible that the train exceeded the speed limit. However, we cannot say for certain that it did — the actual speed could be anywhere between the lower and upper bounds.

10 A triangle has base 8.4 cm and perpendicular height 5.2 cm, each to 1 d.p. Find the upper and lower bounds for the area. (Area = ½ × base × height) Hard ▾
1 d.p. → half-interval = 0.05 cm UB(base) = 8.45 cm LB(base) = 8.35 cm UB(height) = 5.25 cm LB(height) = 5.15 cm Note: ½ is EXACT — no bounds needed for it. UB(area) = ½ × UB(base) × UB(height) = ½ × 8.45 × 5.25 = ½ × 44.3625 = 22.18125 cm² ≈ 22.2 cm² (3 s.f.) LB(area) = ½ × LB(base) × LB(height) = ½ × 8.35 × 5.15 = ½ × 43.0025 = 21.50125 cm² ≈ 21.5 cm² (3 s.f.) Nominal area = ½ × 8.4 × 5.2 = 21.84 cm² UB (22.2) above, LB (21.5) below ✔
