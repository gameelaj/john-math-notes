Simultaneous Equations: Elimination & Substitution

📅 30 April 2026 IGCSE Edexcel · Algebra · Paper 1H & 2H

What's the idea? A simultaneous equation is when you have two equations with two unknowns (usually x and y). One equation alone has infinite solutions — but two equations together pin down one specific pair of values that works for both.

Why it matters: Almost every "real-world" maths problem (mixing prices, ages, rates, intersections of lines) becomes a simultaneous equation. Master this and you unlock half of Paper 2H.

📐 Method 1: Elimination

The big idea: get rid of one variable by adding or subtracting the two equations. Whatever's left is a normal one-variable equation you already know how to solve.

1
Reorder so the variables line up
Both equations should have x on one side, y next to it, and the number on the other side. Same column = same variable.
2
Make the coefficients of x or y the same
Pick the variable that's easier to match. Multiply one (or both) equations by a number until the coefficients match. Multiply every term, not just one!
3
Add or subtract the equations
SSS = Same Sign Subtract. If the matching coefficients have the same sign (both +3y or both −3y) → subtract. If they have different signs (+3y and −3y) → add.
4
Solve the one-variable equation
After step 3, one variable disappears. You're left with something like 5x = 20 → solve normally.
5
Substitute back to find the other variable
Plug the value you found into either original equation. Pick the simpler one.
6
Check by plugging both values into the other equation
30 seconds of checking saves you from losing 4 marks. Always do it.
💡 Memory hook: "Same Signs Subtract" (SSS). If the signs match, subtract. If they're different, add. Whisper "SSS" before step 3 every time.

Worked Examples — Elimination

Easy
Solve: 2x + y = 7 and 3x − y = 8
(1) 2x + y = 7
(2) 3x − y = 8
Already lined up. Look at y: coefficients are +1 and −1.
Different signs → ADD the equations.
(1) + (2): 5x = 15
x = 3
Substitute x = 3 into (1):
2(3) + y = 7 → 6 + y = 7 → y = 1
Check in (2): 3(3) − 1 = 9 − 1 = 8 ✓
x = 3, y = 1
Easy
Solve: 4x + 3y = 18 and 4x − y = 6
Coefficients of x are both +4 — same sign → SUBTRACT.
(1) − (2): (4x − 4x) + (3y − (−y)) = 18 − 6
0 + 4y = 12
y = 3
Sub y = 3 into (2): 4x − 3 = 6 → 4x = 9 → x = 9/4
Watch the double negative: 3y − (−y) = 3y + y = 4y. Don't write 2y!
x = 9/4 (or 2.25), y = 3
Medium
Solve: 3x + 2y = 16 and 5x − 3y = 14
Coefficients don't match. Make y match: multiply (1) by 3 and (2) by 2.
(1) × 3: 9x + 6y = 48
(2) × 2: 10x − 6y = 28
Now y coefficients are +6 and −6. Different signs → ADD.
19x = 76
x = 4
Sub x = 4 into (1): 3(4) + 2y = 16 → 12 + 2y = 16 → y = 2
Check in (2): 5(4) − 3(2) = 20 − 6 = 14 ✓
x = 4, y = 2
Hard — Exam style
A shop sells 4 pens and 3 notebooks for £11. It sells 6 pens and 2 notebooks for £10. Find the price of each.
Let p = pen price, n = notebook price.
(1) 4p + 3n = 11
(2) 6p + 2n = 10
Match n: multiply (1) by 2, (2) by 3.
(1) × 2: 8p + 6n = 22
(2) × 3: 18p + 6n = 30
Same sign → SUBTRACT.
(2) − (1): 10p = 8 → p = 0.80
Sub p = 0.80 into (1): 4(0.80) + 3n = 11 → 3.20 + 3n = 11 → 3n = 7.80 → n = 2.60
Check in (2): 6(0.80) + 2(2.60) = 4.80 + 5.20 = 10 ✓
Pen = £0.80, Notebook = £2.60

🔄 Method 2: Substitution

The big idea: rearrange one equation to make x or y the subject, then plug that expression into the other equation. You replace one letter with an expression — substitution!

1
Pick the easier equation to rearrange
Look for a variable with coefficient 1 (or −1) — it rearranges cleanly with no fractions.
2
Rearrange to make x or y the subject
e.g. y = 2x + 3 (or x = ...). Now you have an expression for that variable.
3
Substitute into the OTHER equation
Don't substitute back into the same equation — you'll get 0 = 0, which is useless. Always sub into the equation you didn't rearrange.
4
Solve the resulting one-variable equation
Expand brackets carefully, collect like terms, solve.
5
Substitute back to find the other variable
Use your rearranged expression from step 2 — that's the quickest way.

Worked Examples — Substitution (Linear)

Easy
Solve: y = 3x − 1 and 2x + y = 9
Equation (1) is already rearranged: y = 3x − 1. Sub into (2).
2x + (3x − 1) = 9
5x − 1 = 9
5x = 10 → x = 2
Sub x = 2 into y = 3x − 1: y = 3(2) − 1 = 5
Check in (2): 2(2) + 5 = 9 ✓
x = 2, y = 5
Medium
Solve: x + 2y = 11 and 3x − y = 5
Equation (1) has x with coefficient 1 — easy to rearrange.
From (1): x = 11 − 2y
Sub into (2):
3(11 − 2y) − y = 5
33 − 6y − y = 5
33 − 7y = 5 → −7y = −28 → y = 4
Sub y = 4 back: x = 11 − 2(4) = 3
Watch: when expanding 3(11 − 2y), the −2y becomes −6y. Easy place to drop a sign.
x = 3, y = 4

🚀 Why substitution is more powerful

Here's the cool part: elimination only works when both equations are linear (just x and y, no x² or xy or anything fancy). But substitution works on non-linear systems too — like when one equation is a curve.

This is huge for IGCSE: questions about a line meeting a circle, or a line meeting a quadratic curve, are guaranteed Paper 2H material. Elimination can't do these. Substitution can.

Worked Examples — Substitution (Non-linear)

Medium
Solve: y = x + 1 and y = x² − 1
Both equations equal y, so set them equal:
x² − 1 = x + 1
x² − x − 2 = 0
(x − 2)(x + 1) = 0
x = 2 or x = −1
Two x-values means two solutions — the line crosses the curve at two points.
When x = 2: y = 2 + 1 = 3
When x = −1: y = −1 + 1 = 0
(2, 3) and (−1, 0)
Hard — Exam style
Solve: x² + y² = 25 and y = x + 1
This is a circle (radius 5) meeting a straight line. Substitute y = x + 1 into the circle equation:
x² + (x + 1)² = 25
Expand carefully: (x + 1)² = x² + 2x + 1, NOT x² + 1.
x² + x² + 2x + 1 = 25
2x² + 2x − 24 = 0
x² + x − 12 = 0 (divided by 2)
(x + 4)(x − 3) = 0
x = −4 or x = 3
Sub each into y = x + 1:
x = −4 → y = −3. x = 3 → y = 4.
Check first pair: (−4)² + (−3)² = 16 + 9 = 25 ✓
(−4, −3) and (3, 4)

⚠️ Common Mistakes John Should Watch For

1. Forgetting to multiply EVERY term. If you multiply equation (1) by 3, every single term gets ×3, including the number on the right side. Easy 2 marks lost.
2. Mixing up add/subtract. Same Signs Subtract (SSS). Different signs add. Whisper SSS before step 3, every time.
3. Substituting back into the wrong equation. When you rearrange equation (1), you must sub into equation (2). Subbing back into (1) just gives 0 = 0 — useless.
4. Squaring brackets wrong. (x + 1)² = x² + 2x + 1, NOT x² + 1. Always expand using FOIL or "smile".
5. Forgetting the second solution in non-linear systems. A line crossing a curve usually meets it at TWO points. If you only give one (x, y) pair, you lose half the marks.

⚡ Quick Recap (30-second review)

  • Elimination: match coefficients → SSS (Same Signs Subtract, different signs add) → solve → sub back.
  • Substitution: rearrange one equation → sub into the OTHER → solve → sub back.
  • Multiply every term when scaling an equation.
  • Substitution is the more powerful method — it handles non-linear systems (line + circle, line + curve).
  • Non-linear systems usually have two solutions (two intersection points). Always check for both.
  • Always verify your answer by plugging both values into the equation you didn't use to solve.
