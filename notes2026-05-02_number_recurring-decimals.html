Recurring Decimals → Exact Fractions

📅 2 May 2026 📌 Edexcel IGCSE — Number 📄 Paper 1H & 2H 🔢 Non-Calculator Skill

💡 What & Why

A recurring decimal is one where a digit (or group of digits) repeats forever — for example 0.333333… or 0.142857142857… These are rational numbers, meaning they can always be written as an exact fraction.

The trick is to use algebra: call the decimal x, multiply to shift the repeating part, then subtract to make the repeating part cancel out. You're left with a simple equation you can solve.

This comes up in Paper 1H and 2H. It's worth 2–3 marks and, once you know the method, it's very reliable — you just have to be careful about how many digits are repeating.

🔵 Dot Notation — Reading the Dots

In IGCSE, a dot above a digit means it repeats. Two dots mark the start and end of a repeating block.

0.3̇
0.3333333… (one digit repeats)
0.1̇8̇
0.181818… (two digits repeat)
0.1̇2̄3̇
0.123123… (three digits repeat)
0.38̇
0.3888… (only the 8 repeats, 3 does NOT)

⚠️ Watch out: 0.38̇ is NOT the same as 0.3̇8̇. In 0.38̇, only the 8 repeats!

🟢 The Method — Step by Step

Step 1 — ACTION
Let x equal the recurring decimal. Write it out with several repeated digits so the pattern is obvious.
Step 2 — RULE
Count how many digits are in the repeating block. Multiply both sides of your equation by:
• 10 if 1 digit repeats
• 100 if 2 digits repeat
• 1000 if 3 digits repeat
This shifts the decimal point to align the repeating parts.
Step 3 — CALCULATE
Subtract the smaller equation from the bigger one. The repeating decimal part cancels out, leaving a whole number equation.
Step 4 — ACTION
Solve for x by dividing both sides by the coefficient of x.
Step 5 — WARNING / CHECK
Always simplify your fraction to its lowest terms! Find the HCF of numerator and denominator and divide both. Then verify by dividing on a calculator.
KEY RULE — Non-Recurring Digits
If some digits before the repeating block do not repeat (like 0.38̇), you need TWO multiplications — one to move past the non-recurring digits, one to align the repeating parts. Then subtract.
EASY Example 1 — One digit repeating (pure recurring)
Convert 0.7̇ (= 0.7777…) into a fraction.
Step 1 — Let x equal the decimal
x = 0.7777777…
Step 2 — 1 digit repeats, so multiply by 10
10 × x = 10 × 0.7777777…
Step 3 — Write both equations clearly
10x = 7.7777777… ← call this equation (2) 1x = 0.7777777… ← call this equation (1)
Step 3 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 10x = 7.7777777… − (1) 1x = 0.7777777… ───────────────── 9x = 7.0000000… 9x = 7
Step 4 — Solve for x
9x = 7 9x ÷ 9 = 7 ÷ 9 x = 7/9
Step 5 — Can we simplify?
HCF of 7 and 9 is 1 (7 is prime, 9 = 3²). The fraction is already in its simplest form.
✓ CHECK: 7 ÷ 9 = 0.7777… ✔
EASY Example 2 — Two digits repeating (pure recurring)
Convert 0.1̇8̇ (= 0.181818…) into a fraction.
Step 1 — Let x equal the decimal
x = 0.181818181818…
Step 2 — 2 digits repeat (18), so multiply by 100
100 × x = 100 × 0.181818…
Step 3 — Write both equations
100x = 18.181818… ← equation (2) 1x = 0.181818… ← equation (1)
Step 3 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 100x = 18.181818… − (1) 1x = 0.181818… ────────────────── 99x = 18.000000… 99x = 18
Step 4 — Solve for x
99x = 18 99x ÷ 99 = 18 ÷ 99 x = 18/99
Step 5 — Simplify the fraction
Find HCF of 18 and 99. 18 = 2 × 3 × 3 99 = 3 × 3 × 11 HCF = 9 18 ÷ 9 = 2 99 ÷ 9 = 11 x = 2/11
✓ CHECK: 2 ÷ 11 = 0.181818… ✔
EASY Example 3 — Three digits repeating
Convert 0.1̇2̇3̇ (= 0.123123123…) into a fraction.
Step 1 — Let x equal the decimal
x = 0.123123123…
Step 2 — 3 digits repeat (123), so multiply by 1000
1000 × x = 1000 × 0.123123123…
Step 3 — Write both equations
1000x = 123.123123… ← equation (2) 1x = 0.123123… ← equation (1)
Step 3 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 1000x = 123.123123… − (1) 1x = 0.123123… ────────────────── 999x = 123.000000… 999x = 123
Step 4 — Solve for x
999x = 123 999x ÷ 999 = 123 ÷ 999 x = 123/999
Step 5 — Simplify the fraction
Find HCF of 123 and 999. 123 = 3 × 41 999 = 3 × 333 = 3 × 3 × 111 = 3 × 3 × 3 × 37 HCF = 3 123 ÷ 3 = 41 999 ÷ 3 = 333 x = 41/333
✓ CHECK: 41 ÷ 333 = 0.123123… ✔
MEDIUM Example 4 — Non-recurring digit before the repeat (e.g. 0.38̇)
Convert 0.38̇ (= 0.3888…) into a fraction. Note: only the 8 repeats, not the 3!
KEY RULE — Why this is different
Because the 3 before the 8 does NOT repeat, we can't just multiply by 10 and subtract. We need to use TWO equations so that when we subtract, the repeating 8s cancel perfectly.
Step 1 — Let x equal the decimal
x = 0.38888888…
Step 2 — Multiply by 100 (to shift past the non-recurring AND the recurring digit)
100 × x = 100 × 0.38888… 100x = 38.8888… ← equation (2)
Step 3 — Multiply by 10 (to shift to just before the recurring digit begins)
10 × x = 10 × 0.38888… 10x = 3.8888… ← equation (1)
Why these two? Look at the .8888… part
In both equations, the decimal part is .8888… — so when we subtract, it cancels!
Step 4 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 100x = 38.8888… − (1) 10x = 3.8888… ─────────────── 90x = 35.0000… 90x = 35
Step 5 — Solve for x
90x = 35 90x ÷ 90 = 35 ÷ 90 x = 35/90
Step 6 — Simplify the fraction
Find HCF of 35 and 90. 35 = 5 × 7 90 = 5 × 18 = 5 × 2 × 9 HCF = 5 35 ÷ 5 = 7 90 ÷ 5 = 18 x = 7/18
✓ CHECK: 7 ÷ 18 = 0.3888… ✔
MEDIUM Example 5 — Two non-recurring digits before the repeat (e.g. 0.41̇6̇)
Convert 0.41̇6̇ (= 0.41666…) into a fraction. The 4 and 1 do NOT repeat; only the 6 repeats.
Step 1 — Let x equal the decimal
x = 0.416666666…
Step 2 — Plan: 2 non-recurring digits, 1 recurring digit
Multiply by 1000 (to get past 2 non-recurring + 1 recurring = 3 digits total) and by 100 (to get past just the 2 non-recurring digits). Then subtract.
Step 3 — Multiply by 1000
1000x = 416.6666… ← equation (2)
Step 4 — Multiply by 100
100x = 41.6666… ← equation (1)
Step 5 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 1000x = 416.6666… − (1) 100x = 41.6666… ──────────────── 900x = 375.0000… 900x = 375
Step 6 — Solve for x
900x = 375 900x ÷ 900 = 375 ÷ 900 x = 375/900
Step 7 — Simplify
Find HCF of 375 and 900. 375 = 3 × 125 = 3 × 5 × 5 × 5 900 = 4 × 225 = 4 × 9 × 25 = 2² × 3² × 5² HCF = 3 × 25 = 75 375 ÷ 75 = 5 900 ÷ 75 = 12 x = 5/12
✓ CHECK: 5 ÷ 12 = 0.41666… ✔
HARD Example 6 — Exam-style: prove that 0.4̇5̄6̇ = 152/333
Show that 0.4̇5̄6̇ = 0.456456456… can be written as the fraction 152/333.
Step 1 — Write out the decimal clearly
x = 0.456456456456…
The repeating block is 456 — three digits repeat. There are no non-recurring digits before the block.
Step 2 — 3 digits repeat, so multiply by 1000
1000 × x = 1000 × 0.456456456…
Step 3 — Write both equations
1000x = 456.456456… ← equation (2) 1x = 0.456456… ← equation (1)
Step 4 — Subtract (1) from (2)
(2) 1000x = 456.456456… − (1) 1x = 0.456456… ────────────────── 999x = 456.000000… 999x = 456
Step 5 — Solve for x
999x = 456 999x ÷ 999 = 456 ÷ 999 x = 456/999
Step 6 — Simplify to check it matches 152/333
Find HCF of 456 and 999. 456 = 2³ × 3 × 19 999 = 3³ × 37 HCF = 3 456 ÷ 3 = 152 999 ÷ 3 = 333 x = 152/333 ✔
Finishing line — write a clear conclusion
Since 456/999 = 152/333, we have shown that 0.4̇5̄6̇ = 152/333. ∎
✓ CHECK: 152 ÷ 333 = 0.456456… ✔
HARD Example 7 — Exam-style multi-step: show 0.8̇3̇ + 0.1̇6̇ = 1
Convert 0.8̇3̇ and 0.1̇6̇ individually into fractions, then add them to show their sum equals 1.
Part A — Convert 0.8̇3̇ (= 0.838383…)
Let p = 0.838383…, two digits repeat → multiply by 100
100p = 83.838383… ← equation (2) 1p = 0.838383… ← equation (1) (2) 100p = 83.838383… − (1) 1p = 0.838383… ────────────────── 99p = 83 p = 83/99
Simplify 83/99
83 is prime. 99 = 9 × 11 = 3² × 11. 83 is not divisible by 3 or 11. HCF = 1 → already in simplest form. p = 83/99
Part B — Convert 0.1̇6̇ (= 0.161616…)
Let q = 0.161616…, two digits repeat → multiply by 100
100q = 16.161616… ← equation (2) 1q = 0.161616… ← equation (1) (2) 100q = 16.161616… − (1) 1q = 0.161616… ────────────────── 99q = 16 q = 16/99
Part C — Add the two fractions
p + q = 83/99 + 16/99 = (83 + 16) / 99 = 99/99 = 1 ✔
✓ SHOWN: 0.8̇3̇ + 0.1̇6̇ = 83/99 + 16/99 = 99/99 = 1 ✔

🚨 Common Mistakes — Watch Out, John!

❌
Multiplying by the wrong power of 10: Count the digits in the repeating block carefully. If 2 digits repeat, multiply by 100, not 10. Getting this wrong means the .888… parts don't line up and don't cancel.
❌
Treating 0.38̇ like 0.3̇8̇: In 0.38̇, only the 8 repeats. The 3 is a fixed digit. You need TWO multiplications (×100 and ×10). A very common exam trap.
❌
Forgetting to simplify: 18/99 is correct but not simplified — the answer should be 2/11. Always find the HCF and divide both top and bottom.
❌
Subtracting the wrong way round: Always subtract the SMALLER equation from the BIGGER one. Doing it the other way gives you a negative number for 99x, which causes sign errors.
❌
Writing x = 9 instead of 9x = 7: After subtracting, the left-hand side is still 9x, not x. You still have to divide. Don't jump to the answer.
❌
Confusing 0.9̇ ≠ 1: Actually 0.9̇ = 0.9999… = 1 exactly (not approximately). This is a famous mathematical fact. Using the method: 10x − x = 9x = 9, so x = 1. Don't be surprised if this comes up!

⚡ Quick Recap — 30 Second Review

  • Let x = the decimal. Write out enough repeated digits so the pattern is visible.
  • Multiply by 10ⁿ where n = number of digits in the repeating block (10, 100, or 1000).
  • Subtract the smaller equation from the bigger. The repeating part cancels out.
  • Solve: divide both sides by the coefficient of x.
  • If there are non-recurring digits before the repeat, use TWO multiplications then subtract them.
  • Always simplify the fraction — find HCF and divide numerator and denominator by it.

7 Show that 0.9̇ = 1 exactly (not approximately). Medium ▾
Let x = 0.9999… 1 digit repeats → multiply by 10 10x = 9.9999… ← (2) 1x = 0.9999… ← (1) (2) 10x = 9.9999… − (1) 1x = 0.9999… ───────────── 9x = 9.0000… 9x = 9 9x ÷ 9 = 9 ÷ 9 x = 9/9 x = 1 ∴ 0.9̇ = 1 (exactly, not approximately!)

This surprises everyone at first! But the algebra is correct. 0.9̇ and 1 are two ways of writing the same number — just like 1/2 and 2/4.
